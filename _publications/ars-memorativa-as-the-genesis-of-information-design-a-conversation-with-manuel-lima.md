Manuel Lima is one of the most prominent figures of data visualization since the publication of _Visual Complexity_ (Lima 2011). In this conversation, Manuel Lima traces back the origin of data visualization to Ars Memorativa, an ancient mnemonic technique to organize information and facilitate its recall. Going back to the origins is an obsession that brought him to collect and arrange into books images of information design from both physical and digital archives. By doing this, Manuel Lima tackled issues related to digital objects and their creation, use, and preservation, with a point of view capable of combining the passion for visualizing information and the profession of UX designer.

<!--more-->

This conversation, which took place between Lisbon and Milan on Wednesday 28 July 2021, comes from a blurb that Manuel Lima wrote for _Mapping Affinities_ (Rodighiero 2021). The discussion is part of the project From Data to Wisdom, and is supported by Fundação para a Ciência e a Tecnologia through the grant POCI-01-0145-FEDER-029717 and the Swiss National Science Foundation through the grant 194442. This text, originally created for the forthcoming book _From Data to Wisdom_ (Higuera Rubio et al. 2022), is published as a preview for _Nightingale_, the journal of the Data Visualization Society.

## How It All Started

Manuel Lima's love of data visualization started during his Master of Fine Arts at Parsons School of Design in New York, a two-year program. During that period, a teacher gave a lecture on information architecture and presented a diagram called the Understanding Spectrum (Shedroff [1994] 1999), which basically shows how data leads into information, information into knowledge, and knowledge ultimately into wisdom (see Figure 1). Even though his background is actually in industrial design and product design, he was just super interested in being part of the process of bridging information and knowledge. That is the key challenge, arguably one of the most critical challenges of the century. He immediately started planning different projects, already thinking about data visualization in an attempt to make sense and visualize systems.

He remembers doing a project on visualizing both a computer virus and a human virus, a topic that has become urgent for all of us due to COVID-19. He got really into epidemiology and the dreaded diseases. He was fascinated by how the spread of a computer virus is actually similar to a human virus, contaminating hubs within a network. At the time, he was reading Albert Barabási's _Linked_ (Barabási 2002) and Steven Johnson's _Emergence_ (Johnson 2001). This slowly got him into networks, and he became obsessed with these structures.

For his thesis, Lima was trying to build a visualization tool to understand how information spreads within blogs. This was fifteen years ago and blogs were very recent. Blogs were just coming out and everyone was saying, “It's the end of the media as we know it.” But he was really more interested in how blogs themselves became a social laboratory for investigation, which is something that social scientists have been trying to do for ages. Today we have this perfect lab to understand how information spreads from point A to point B, which people are “contaminated” by this information, and what the overall pattern of dissemination looks like.

While doing this research, Lima started collecting examples of network visualization, which intrigued him because they underlie the structure of the blogosphere and the world. His obsessive-compulsive nature was pushing him to collect examples not just from that specific domain, but from everything: biological networks, computer systems, and social networks. That was really the genesis. Once he graduated, he felt the body of research was too good to keep to himself, and that is how the book started. As he was collecting some of those examples on visual complexity, he realized this would be a great book because several of the examples were actually, unfortunately, disappearing. The book was serving multiple purposes — he published _Visual Complexity_ (Lima 2011) five years after the website — but one reason was to preserve digital maps for future generations because many of them were disappearing. _Visual Complexity_ is a way of preserving some of that content.

Then, his curiosity about the origin of things prompted him to write his two other books. He was always trying to see who was the first person to think about network diagrams or create a network diagram, and that led him to investigate tree diagrams, the topic of his second book, _The Book of Trees_ (Lima 2014). The same mental process of going back to the origins, in trying to understand the very first diagrams that humans created, inspired him to write about the circle (Lima 2017).

## From Contemporary to Ancient Visuals

In _The Book of Trees_ and _The Book of Circles_ there is an implicit attempt to demystify the idea that we are at the pinnacle stage of civilization, and that data visualization is a brand new thing that has emerged in the last hundred years as if nothing had happened before. Lima hopes his two books displace this misconception because it is simply not true. We have been doing this for centuries, if not millennia. Now we just have better tools and more data, but the challenge is still the same: to make the invisible visible and to find patterns in information.

Even the sense of being overwhelmed with information, which is such a common thing today, is also not completely new. There is a book titled _Too Much to Know_ (Blair 2010) that Lima absolutely loves because it details the anxiety that medieval people were facing with the avalanche of information coming from ancient Rome and Greece. There was all this incredible explosion of content, and people had to make sense of it. Developments in printing then increased book production. There is this sense that we were creating so much knowledge, so much information that it was impossible for any single human to digest. There was a similar type of anxiety to the one we have today with big data, that there is too much information out there.

## Ars Memorativa as the Genesis of Information Design

Lima always goes back to what is known as Ars Memorativa, also called the Art of Memory in English. Ars Memorativa is a set of mnemonic techniques used by scholars back then — most of them religious because that was the nature of knowledge — to memorize information and recall it at later times. Lima does think that Ars Memorativa is the genesis of information design.

Since Ancient Greece, the great thinkers of the past have defined a lot of critical design principles that, even today, are still used and embraced by interaction design, UX design, and information design. Ideas like chunking and hierarchy were defined during the Ars Memorativa movement.

It was during this time, when the avalanche of information was coming from ancient Rome and Greece, that some people realized that visual communication and diagrammatic communication were notable ways to deal with this data. There were outliers, a few people who wanted to have all the information in a diagrammatic form because it was just easier to consume. They were intentional about their goals: diagrams were meant not to accompany the text but to replace it altogether. This was really the birth of Ars Memorativa that shaped information design as we know it today, roughly nine centuries ago.

## User Experience and Information Design

The relation between user experience and information design is a question Lima runs into all the time. Looking back, his professional life was divided into two parallel tracks. On the one hand, he was doing research, lectures, and teaching related to data visualization and information design. On the other hand, his 9:00–5:00 job was working for big companies like Google and Microsoft on interaction design and UX design. Though they were parallel tracks, he has always felt that they were pretty connected. User experience design and data visualization are interconnected through interaction. One thing that really differentiates modern-day information design is the requirement of interactive design. Otherwise, we fall back into traditional graphic design on paper. Once you embrace interaction, once you are creating digital experiences that allow users to interact, filter, and drill down different aspects of a visualization, you inevitably enter the domain of interaction design and user experience, which is all about understanding the flow, usability, accessibility, and things of that nature. There is a huge overlap. Interaction is the glue between user experience design and data visualization.

## Reaching Readers Through Books

It is always hard to pinpoint the causality of certain things in life. Lima's books definitely led to more lectures, more invitations for events, and speaking arrangements. The TED talk came through _The Book of Trees_: one of the recruiters for TED saw the book and invited him to give a lecture.

One inevitable effect was more speaking engagements. But first of all, writing is almost a compulsion to just put it out. Lima is living with this thing, and he has to put it out. He cannot live with himself if he just does not put it out into the world. There is the same sense of need that other writers have conveyed. For Lima, the second thing is really touching people. What happens when you write a book like that is that you touch other people without even getting to know them. That is really the main reason he does it. He loves it when he gets an email, a message, or a LinkedIn post from someone saying, “I read this book and it changed my life,” or “I changed careers because of this book.” It is fun. Due to the nature of the work he ends up doing in places like Google and Microsoft, the output is really ephemeral. He likes the digital environment — it is always changing — but if he looks back, there is really nothing he can show his kids. Whatever software he created has been replaced; it is already long gone. The books are at least one way of lasting a little bit longer, of keeping something that will hopefully outlast him, and that future generations can consume it.

## The Fragility of Digital Objects

Digital preservation is a huge problem. It is really something that more of us should be worried about if we care about culture and preservation for the future. Many of our current digital creations are just vanishing as we speak.

When Lima was doing research for _Visual Complexity_, it was easier to find a medieval illustration created eight centuries ago than a digital execution created ten or twenty years ago. The reasons are really multifaceted. Sometimes the plugin does not work anymore; Adobe Flash does not exist anymore, it was abandoned. You have to assume that the authors or the companies still exist and are diligently keeping the image or the code somewhere. It is not on a server anymore — it is probably on a local drive, who knows? These digital objects just keep on vanishing. They keep on deteriorating, and we are losing them. In a few cases you still have images, but the resolution is so tiny they could not even be included in books.

There is this notion that the digital will save us. It is really dangerous because we do not realize how ephemeral the digital really is — how unsafe it is. It is prone to every type of attack. We are seeing this all over the world today. Digital is no guarantee of preservation; it is likely less of a guarantee than paper or stone. When Lima goes to the British Museum, he sees incredible examples of preservation in cuneiform texts from Babylonian tablets dating back five thousand years. Some papers do not even last that long. Can we really look back in eight centuries and see anything done today using our digital tools? Lima has strong doubts about that. Rodighiero recognized the same fragility in his own work: his master's thesis project, created in Flash, can no longer be opened, and only the code and some screenshots survive in his cloud storage.

## A New Book, a New Life

Lima's new book is going to be different. His visual phase has ended, and this book likely will not have a single image. It is going to be more focused on design — on the responsibility of design in this new world. The book will cover ethics, environmental responsibility, social responsibility, and topics of that nature. It will be around nine chapters, and each chapter is meant to demystify a preconceived idea that we might have about design.

The new direction is also connected to a recent change of life. Lima spent the last ten years in New York and the last twenty years outside of Portugal, where he is originally from, and it has been quite a journey. With two small daughters, Chloe and Caniba, the family found themselves locked in a two-bedroom apartment in the midst of COVID-19. It was crazy to see New York City just closed. The schools were closed; it was just too much, and Lima realized it was time to go. The family came to Lisbon in June 2020, and they had an amazing summer. Portugal back then was completely open; it was almost like life was normal. That was really, really good.

The feelings are mixed. New York City will always be a city the family loves, and they now have dual citizenship — Lima is Portuguese-American — so it is sure to remain part of their lives. But so far, Lisbon is really fun: the weather, the food, the beaches; it is hard to beat.
